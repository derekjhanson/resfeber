Wanaka

By DJH June 29, 2019

 

She didn't want to get up. She knew what was coming. She'd seen the mountains in the fading sun as we pulled into town the night before. Admittedly, they look intimidating. 

Some mountains go on forever, casually climbing to their heights, others race straight for the sky. These were skyscraper mountains.

Yet, a dense morning fog kept the peaks from view. Worried the blanket might extend all the way to the top, we nevertheless managed to leave our holiday park and make our way to the trailhead. 

Looking up, we could see reddish-orange puffs of grass (red tussock) in the frosty air, patches of leafy green succulents with thick, bumpy surfaces like green sandpaper, and a few unknown brushes poking up through the grassland. The chill air and thick fog kept our destination from view, leaving us to trod up the mountain, unclear of how far we truly had to go.

The fog gave the land the appearance of midwinter, the time at which the cold and lack of sunlight move from being a nuisance to feeling unbearable. Yet the sound of songbirds and occasional quail crossing the trail served to lift our spirits as we walked. And walked. And slipped. And walked more.
