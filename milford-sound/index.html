Milford Sound

DJH

 

The morning cruise was calm and quiet. The bathrooms foretold a different story in the summer months (picture a wall of 50 urinals to choose from). The valley receives almost daily rainfall, meaning the vast majority of tours take place under a blanket of clouds, with limited visibility. Most tourists don't mind, as the rain provides a series of waterfalls cascading down from the peaks and glaciers above. But, to get to watch the sun slowly rise over the peaks and see the full sound throughout the tour was a wonderful experience (I think I prefer the sunny day tour). I mean, there are still several beautiful waterfalls within the sound for viewing, even on dry days (with glaciers melting even in the winter today).

The sheer size of the sound is hard to fathom in photographs. 

The Encounter Nature Cruise was the longest offered in the winter season, so we signed up!

Mitre Peak stands watch over the land entrance to the sound, with boats similar to ours barely visible in the distance.

The boat meanders around the sound, often pulling right up next to the walls to get a closer look at the trees, the darker than dark water, and the sealife within the visible top layers (including starfish with eight arms).

The cliffs are interesting rock faces which glisten with color in the sunlight. The rocks at the edge of the sound give a glimpse into their composition.

Stirling Falls teases the eye from a distance for the first quarter of the trip, gradually coming closer and closer as you make your way out towards the open sea.

The colors change throughout the morning as the sun alters the appearance of the landscape. Full of shadows and dark greys in the early morning, a blueish-green takes over soon after, highlighting the vegetation which covers every possible inch of the steep cliffs.

The boat we chose offered indoor and outdoor seating, with minimal movement on the smooth morning waters. While the deck clearly afforded the best views, the chilling temperatures and incredibly cold breeze off the Tasman Sea were far from ideal. Though it did mean a nearly empty outdoor area to move about and take in the incredible views!

Upon reaching the open sea the waters quickly turn a brilliant light blue, the sun can be seen with all its splendor, the water becomes choppy, and the wind seems to pull the gloves off your hands. Though the entrance to the sound may be its most striking feature.

The return trip included passing by a favority resting place of New Zealand Fur Seals on the way to an up close encounter with the waterfall we'd been watching all morning.

For brave, hearty souls, the tour offers a 'glacial facial'. You can stand at the front of the boat as it sails into the cascade of water from the glacier above. My kind of facial!

After getting doused with bone-chillingly cold water, the tour is nearly over. After arriving back at the dock, we disembarked and headed over for overpriced lunch, though with the view of the sound included, perhaps it was underpriced!
