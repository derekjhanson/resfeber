Rotorua

DJH

At the foot of a large lake, and with plenty of geothermal activity right under your toes, Rotorua has something for you rain or shine. It's been a dream of mine to make and enjoy my very own temporary hot spring pool next to a lake or ocean. New Zealand can make that happen, and upon arrival at our holiday park I learned the lake has a hot springs beach, just steps away. My dream come true!

The makeshift path, right past gurgling mud pots and thermal vents on both sides, feels as though it would never exist in litigious America. Surely someone has received a scalding walking this trail in the dark. Dangers aside, we made it to the beach, ready to dig in and enjoy the warmth and a view of the lake.

Our intentions changed quickly. While the water is certainly warm, it isn't quite inviting.

Not a problem though, because the town is full of naturally fed thermal pools without the 'fun' that can go with truly natural springs. We soaked our cares away in the naturally fed hot tubs and planned our next day in the trees.

Whakarewarewa

South of the lake lies a large redwood forest. Trails wind around the original plantings, up and down hillsides, and in-and-out of the thick woods. Often rainy, or at the very least foggy from the level of geothermal activity nearby, a hike in the woods seems perfect for the weather. The ground-level hikes and mountain bike trails wrap through these lush stands of redwoods with heights and girth which belie their actual age.

Brought over from America, redwoods were introduced to New Zealand for timber. With a climate similar to areas where such trees flourish in California, the redwoods grew well. In fact, the climate in this forest is even more amenable for quick redwood growth than anticipated. The additional rainfall, consistent fog, and stable temperatures have led to trees in this forest reaching heights near some of their far more aged American counterparts.

While still forested for timber, the Whakarewarewa forest is also an eco-tourist hotspot. With towering redwoods and plentiful ferns of several varieties, including the silver fern of the All Blacks.

Meandering through the forest is a geothermal stream. It is filled with fern leaves and debris common to a forest floor, but the color of the water is spectacular. A beautiful reminder of the geologic forces constantly at work underneath New Zealand (Aotearoa).

If enjoying nature from the ground just isn't satisfying enough, an incredible canopy walk has been constructed with over 700 meters of trails up to 65 feet in the air. The supports are all cantilevered and constructed such that the trees can continue to grow and expand, without inhibition by the platform. The engineering alone is worth the hike!

Back on the ground the trails continue through forests with other tree varieties. There are ferns for days, of all shapes, sizes, and heights.

If you take the longest trail (Pohaturoa Track, in yellow on the map below) there are a few viewpoints overlooking Rotorua and the Maori village of Te Puia, though seeing through the geothermal steam in the winter may be a challenge! Note: that 2 hours may be for the extremely fit native New Zealander.
